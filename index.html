Phase 1 · Weeks 1-2
Balance & Athletic Posture
Before Owen throws a single pitch, he needs to understand what balance actually means in pitching — and feel it in his body.
Phase name
Phase goal

🎯 What does balance in pitching actually mean?

Balance in pitching is not about standing perfectly still like a statue. It is about loading and moving your weight in a way that is controlled, repeatable, and directed toward the plate. Most young pitchers who throw hard like Owen already have athletic instincts — but those instincts have never been given a framework. This is that framework. The three things balance means for Owen right now: 1. A stable post leg. The back leg (right leg for a RHP) needs to be a strong, stable platform that Owen loads his weight into before driving forward. If that leg is soft or wobbly, everything downstream falls apart. 2. Weight loaded back — not tilted sideways. When Owen lifts his knee and goes into his balance point, his weight should be loaded directly over his back ankle and heel — not leaning toward third base or first base. Sideways lean is energy lost. 3. Head centered and still. Owen's head is the center of mass for his upper body. If his head drifts or tilts during the delivery, his arm and release point drift with it. John: watch Owen's cap brim. It should move forward toward the plate — not bob up, down, or sideways. Where should Owen's weight be at setup? At the start of his delivery (before the knee lift), Owen's weight should be roughly 50/50 between both feet, centered over the balls of his feet — not the heels, not the toes. As he loads into his kick, 80-90% of that weight transfers to the back leg. That loaded back leg is the spring. If the spring is wobbly, there is no pop. The cue John can use: Ask Owen to feel like he is sitting very slightly into his back hip as he lifts his knee — not squatting down, just a subtle settling in. That is the load. Then everything after that is about driving forward off of it.

Phase 2 · Weeks 3-4
Hip Load & Drive Direction
Owen learns to load his back hip and drive linearly toward home plate — not sideways, not straight down.
Phase name
Phase goal
1Hip to hip drill
John + Owen▾
Reps/sets: 3x8 reps
Cue: Back hip drives to front hip. Front foot lands pointing toward the plate. No rushing the upper half.
John stands behind Owen and places both hands lightly on Owen's hips. As Owen drives toward the plate, John feels whether the movement is linear (forward) or lateral (sideways). This instant feedback is something no mirror can replicate.
  1. Owen starts in balance position, knee raised
  2. John places hands lightly on both sides of Owen's hips from behind
  3. Owen drives down the mound (or forward on flat ground) toward the plate
  4. John feels: is the hip moving forward, or rotating sideways too early?
  5. Goal: both hips should move forward together before any rotation begins

Phase 3 · Weeks 5-6
Arm Path & External Rotation
Establish a clean, repeatable arm path through Owen's high cock position — without reaching back or short-arming.
Phase name
Phase goal
1Seated arm circles — wrist to ear path
Owen solo▾
Reps/sets: 3x10 each arm
Cue: Thumb down on the way back. Thumb up (pointing behind you) at full external rotation. Elbow never drops below shoulder height.
Sitting removes the lower half completely so Owen's arm action gets full attention. This drill builds awareness of where the ball needs to travel before it can accelerate forward. This video below has a bunch of drills, including this one. Owen can do them all on his own!
  1. Owen sits on a bucket, chair, or cooler
  2. Start with ball at hip pocket — thumb pointing down toward the ground
  3. Circle the ball up and back, feeling it rotate into full external rotation (thumb pointing behind, elbow up)
  4. Return the arm forward and simulate a pitch release
  5. John watches from the side: elbow should stay at or above shoulder height throughout

2Scarecrow drill
John + Owen▾

The glove side is the half of Owen's delivery that nobody has ever talked to him about. Train it to be an active anchor — not a passenger.

Phase goal
1Glove tuck drill
John + Owen▾
Reps/sets: 3x10 reps
Cue: The glove arm pulls actively to Owen's chest — not the hip, not swinging out. The glove elbow should drive toward the target before tucking in.
Most young pitchers let their glove arm fly out or collapse limply. The glove should pull firmly against the chest as the throwing arm fires through. John watches from in front.

  2. Drives toward the plate as normal — but this time focusing entirely on the glove arm
  3. As the front foot plants, Owen actively pulls his glove elbow tight to his chest
  4. John watches from in front: does the glove end up pressed against the body, or did it swing wide?
  5. Common fault: glove arm wings out to the side — cue Owen to imagine pulling a drawer shut with his elbow

Phase 5 · Weeks 9-10
Release Point & Follow-Through
Find Owen's consistent release window and train his arm to decelerate safely through a complete, balanced finish.
Phase name
Phase goal
1Fence finish drill
Owen solo▾
Reps/sets: 3x8 reps
Cue: Owen's throwing hand finishes on the outside of his lead leg — not across his body. No wrist flipping. Full arm arc.
Owen stands 10-15 feet from a chain-link fence or a soft net and throws into it at 60% effort. The proximity forces a clean arm path and teaches safe deceleration because there is no distance to generate velocity for.
  1. Owen stands 10-15 feet from a fence or soft net
  2. Throws at 60% effort with a full delivery
  3. After the release, his throwing arm should arc naturally and finish outside and below his lead leg
  4. If his arm brushes the fence on the follow-through, his path is clean
  5. John watches from the side: does Owen stop his arm early, or does it complete the full arc?

🧠 Mindset note — for John & Jen

Phase 6 · Weeks 11-12
Full Delivery Integration
Connect all five previous phases into one fluid, repeatable delivery. Introduce structured flat-ground bullpens with John calling cues.
Phase name
Phase goal
13-checkpoint full delivery
Owen solo▾
Reps/sets: 10 reps to open every session
Cue: (1) Balance at knee lift. (2) Direction at front foot plant. (3) Full finish. Three checkpoints. One continuous motion.
Owen goes through his full delivery but pauses for one full second at each of three checkpoints before continuing. This builds deliberate mechanical sequencing before he attempts a live, full-speed delivery.

  2. Drive through front foot plant — pause 1 second (John checks: did the foot land on line, slightly closed?)
  3. Complete the delivery and hold the finish for 2 seconds
  4. John calls out the checkpoint result: good / adjust
  5. After 10 checkpoint reps, do 5 full continuous deliveries with no pauses

Mindset, Routine & Competition Prep
Build the mental framework that allows Owen to carry his mechanics from flat-ground practice to the mound under real game pressure.
Phase name
Phase goal
1Pressure simulation bullpen
John + Owen▾
Reps/sets: 15 pitches structured like a game
Cue: John calls a count. John calls the situation. John gives zero extra encouragement mid-pitch. Owen must manage himself.
Removes the safety net of a relaxed practice bullpen. John creates game conditions — runs a count, calls out situations, adds natural pressure with silence. The nervous system learns to stay regulated under stress only by actually experiencing stress in a safe environment.
  1. John calls a count and situation before each pitch: full count, runner on third
  2. Owen goes through his full pre-pitch routine before every single pitch
  3. After a ball: John says nothing. Owen must use his reset ritual and reload on his own.
  4. After the 15 pitches, debrief together: what did Owen's body feel like on good pitches vs. bad pitches?
  5. John gives one mechanical observation and one mental observation — not more

🧠 Mindset note — for John & Jen

Mechanics without mindset are a car with no driver. This phase is not optional and is not softer than the physical phases. John and Jen — Owen's ability to reset after a bad pitch, stay present, and compete with his mechanics intact is the whole point of the program.

  • Outcome detachment: after every bullpen, Owen rates the session by mechanical quality on a 1-10 scale — not by where the ball went. This rewires what success means.
  • Mistake ritual: pick one physical reset with Owen — a toe tap, a breath, a glove clap — and use it after every bad pitch without exception. The same ritual every time.
  • Identity script: have Owen write three sentences starting with I am a pitcher who... and read them before every session. Identity precedes behavior.

Owen's Game Day Routine

Two scenarios: Owen is starting • Owen might come in to pitch

Real world setup. Owen arrives with the team roughly 30 minutes before first pitch. John and Jen are in the stands. The warmup happens with teammates and coaches — not with family. These routines are designed to work inside that window and inside a youth team environment. Owen owns this. He knows what to do and when to do it regardless of what anyone else on the team is doing.
T−30 min
Arrive
Mental prep — before anything physical
Get his head right first
Before Owen touches a ball, he takes 60 seconds to himself. Not standing around with teammates yet. Just him.
  • One slow breath — in through nose, out through mouth
  • His 2 mechanical focus cues for today (decided before he got here)
  • Visualize one good pitch — see it, feel it, release it clean in his mind
🧠 Owen solo — 60 seconds
T−28 min
Physical activation — 6 minutes
Dynamic warmup with the team
Owen does the team warmup but treats it seriously. No static stretching. He needs his heart rate up and his body temperature raised before any arm work.
  • Light jog — 2 laps minimum, not a stroll
  • High knees, butt kicks, leg swings — go through every one fully
  • Arm circles — 10 small forward, 10 small back, 10 large each way
  • Trunk rotations — 10 each side, hands on hips, full rotation
  • Hip circles — 10 each direction. Hips are everything. Wake them up.
👥 With team
T−22 min
Arm activation — 5 minutes
Quick arm care before throwing
Owen does this while teammates are getting loose. Takes 4–5 minutes and protects his arm from going zero to game speed too fast.
  • Wrist and elbow circles — 10 each direction
  • Arm huggers — 10 reps, arms swing around body in a hugging motion
  • Flat-back arm swings — bend at waist, back parallel to ground, swing arms so fingertips brush the grass — 10 reps
  • Slow scarecrow rotations — 8 each arm (thumb down back, thumb up at top)
👤 Owen solo
T−17 min
Throwing progression — 8 minutes
Build the arm up in stages with a teammate
Owen grabs a teammate (ideally his catcher) and builds up gradually. He does not start at full distance. He does not skip steps even if other guys are throwing harder sooner.
  • 30 feet — 8 throws: Wrist flips only. Focus on clean backspin off the fingertips. Easy.
  • 45 feet — 8 throws: Add the elbow. Short arm, easy tempo. Watch the spin.
  • 60 feet — 8 throws: Full arm action at 65–70% effort. This is where he practices mechanics, not velocity. One cue in his head.
  • 90 feet — 6 throws: Extend out, full arm path, let it breathe. No forcing.
  • Cut back to 60 feet — 5 throws: These should feel easy after 90. Lock in the feel.
👥 With teammate or catcher
T−9 min
Bullpen — 6 minutes
Mound warmup with catcher — 12–15 pitches
Owen treats this like it counts. Full pre-pitch routine on every single pitch. He is not just lobbing the ball up there. He is finding his release point, his rhythm, and his feel. Keep it under 15 pitches — he needs arm left for the game.
Pitch sequence
4–5 fastballs (both sides of plate)
2–3 change-ups glove side
2 breaking balls if he throws one
2 fastballs to finish — locate them
Owen's mindset here
Full pre-pitch routine every pitch. Reset after each one. If a pitch misses, flush it immediately. The bullpen tells him where his arm is — it does not predict how he'll pitch.
⚾ Bullpen mound with catcher
T−3 min
Final reset — 60 seconds
Go inward before going out
Owen does not need to be hyped up. He needs to be calm and ready. One breath, two cues, walk to the mound like he belongs there.
  • One slow breath — in through nose, out through mouth
  • His 2 focus cues — say them quietly to himself
  • First batter: what's the plan? Attack early in the count.

Game starts
In the dugout
Be a teammate — but stay switched on mentally
Owen cheers his team, stays engaged in the game, and tracks the situation every inning. He is not waiting around passively. He is watching how the starter is throwing, what pitches are working, what the umpire is calling.

Coach signals
You might be in
Bullpen activation — 5–7 minutes
Quick bullpen — he may have 1 inning to get ready
When the coach signals, Owen does not panic. He has a plan. He goes to the bullpen and follows the same quick warmup sequence every time — regardless of how much time he has.

⚾ Game mound
If he never gets in
The hardest scenario
Not pitching is part of pitching
Some games Owen will warm up and never come in. This is genuinely one of the harder mental tests in baseball. The way he handles it tells him — and his coaches — a lot about his character and maturity as a pitcher.
  • Be a good teammate regardless. His energy in the dugout matters.
  • Do not sulk visibly — coaches notice that more than they notice the pitching
  • After the game: one sentence to himself — "I was ready." That is enough.
  • Next practice: back to the work. The arm got protected. That is a win.

🧠 What John and Jen can do from the stands

Your job on game day is simpler than it feels: cheer effort, not outcomes. If Owen throws a bad inning, the most powerful thing you can do is look calm in the stands. He will look for you. What he sees on your face matters more than you think.

Save all mechanical observations for the next day. After a game — good or bad — Owen needs to decompress before he can receive any coaching. A 24-hour rule is not a cliche. It is genuinely how the brain processes performance. The conversation will be better for waiting.

If Owen looks frustrated on the mound, trust the routine. He has it. The program has given him the tools to reset. Let him use them.
